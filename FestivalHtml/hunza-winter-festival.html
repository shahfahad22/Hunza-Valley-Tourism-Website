Welcome to Hunza Winter Festival

Discover the colors and culture of Hunza's festivals

Overview

The Hunza Winter Festival is a lively cultural celebration held during the coldest months of winter in the Hunza Valley. It celebrates the spirit of winter, promotes local traditions, and revives ancient sports and customs practiced by mountain communities for centuries. It represents survival, togetherness, and heritage in one of the most beautiful yet harsh environments on earth.

Timing

Usually celebrated between January and February, during the peak snowfall season. The dates are flexible based on snowfall and local planning. The festival can last for several days or even a week.

Main Celebration Spots

Celebrations occur across Hunza, especially in Altit, Ganish, Karimabad, Aliabad, Upper Hunza (Gojal), Attabad Lake, Passu, and Gulmit.

Purpose of the Festival

  • To unite communities during the harsh winter.
  • To revive traditional winter sports and games.
  • To promote eco and winter tourism in Hunza.
  • To showcase music, food, dress, and daily life of Hunza to the world.
  • To bring joy and mental relief during long, dark winter months.

Main Activities and Events

  • Traditional Winter Sports:
    • Ishkoman Polo (Snow Polo): Played on frozen fields with wooden sticks and soft balls.
    • Buzkashi-style games: Horsemen compete to grab and throw objects.
    • Ice Hockey & Skating: Especially popular in Upper Hunza on frozen lakes.
    • Yak and Donkey Races: Raced along snow-covered paths.
  • Tug of War and Wrestling: Community teams compete in snow-based tug-of-war and traditional "Gushtī" wrestling.
  • Bonfires & Night Events: People gather around fires to tell folk stories, sing traditional songs, and drink butter tea (Gur Gur Chaay). Fire dances are performed by local youth.
  • Music and Dance Shows: Men perform sword and circle dances, while women wear traditional dresses and join in musical shows. Instruments used include Dadang (drum), Surnai (pipe), and Rubab (string instrument).
  • Food Festival: Features dishes like:
    • Harissa – slow-cooked meat with wheat
    • Giyaling – flatbread with butter and honey
    • Hoilo Garma – dry meat with wheat
    • Prapu – noodles in walnut paste
    Served with warm drinks like mulberry juice, herbal teas, and apricot broths.
  • Ice Climbing & Trekking: Guided treks and climbs for tourists and locals, including training on safety and survival.

Symbolic and Social Meaning

The festival transforms winter from a time of isolation to one of joy and strength. It strengthens community ties, passes cultural skills to youth, and replaces darkness with hope and energy.

Modern-Day Significance

Supported by NGOs, local communities, and tourism bodies. It promotes sustainable, cultural tourism and creates income for locals through crafts, food, and guiding services.

Tourist Experience

Visitors are warmly welcomed. They participate in cooking classes, folk dance lessons, and enjoy events like snow sculpture contests and storytelling nights.

Educational Value

Schools arrange exhibitions and activities to teach how ancient communities thrived in extreme winters using unity, wisdom, and creativity.

Conclusion

The Hunza Winter Festival is not just about snow—it's about spirit. Through music, fire, food, and games, the community keeps its culture alive and celebrates resilience in one of the most magical seasons of the year.
